# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Cosimo products are for producers with real chops working in modern electronic and forward-leaning genres. They sit between serious bedroom producer and touring artist: technically fluent, taste-driven, and impatient with tools that make them do low-value setup work. They came up on Ableton, admire Bitwig, own tools such as OTT, Serum, Output libraries, and Spitfire libraries, and can think in scale degrees and chord qualities instead of only raw MIDI notes.

The unifying trait is chops and taste, not income. These users know enough theory and signal processing to value technical depth, but they do not want to waste time tweaking presets or becoming sound designers when the tool could encode the right production judgment for them.

## Product Purpose

The company name is still TBD. It builds music software: VST plugins and an iOS DAW. The throughline is embedded music-production expertise. The founder is a producer and mixer, and that knowledge should be encoded directly into the products at a higher level than competitors.

Every product should be a focused, opinionated improvement on something average that the founder uses daily. The iOS DAW should speak fluent functional harmony instead of treating phone music-making as raw MIDI entry or voice-memo capture. OTTLab should refine Ableton's classic OTT preset by reintroducing dynamics through envelope following. Cosimo Synth and related plugin interfaces should remove steps from serious production workflows without taking control away from the user.

Success means a skilled producer sees the interface and understands that the product contains real music-production judgment. The product should demonstrate the claim through its behavior, screenshots, and technical affordances, not through inflated marketing language.

## Brand Personality

Disciplined. Pragmatic. Modern.

These words describe the posture behind the product. They should not appear as self-description in UI or marketing copy. The brand never declares its character; it demonstrates it through the work.

The voice is short, technical where needed, and confident without performing warmth, intellectualism, heritage, irreverence, or accessibility. Humor and personality are allowed, but not as the headline. They belong in small moments after the product has already earned trust.

The parent brand should feel like quiet, contemporary marketing-as-product-documentation in the lane of Linear and Auxy: the product is the visual proof, the thesis is on the tin, and the page uses exactly as much surface as the job requires. Individual products can have stronger character in the Sugar Bytes or Baby Audio Comeback Kid sense, but they should still feel like distinct objects from the same maker.

The governing visual idea is the Sorayama principle: old craft fused into new form. Use late-80s and early-90s pop-tech references as accents inside modern UI, not as full pastiche. A single retro-rendered icon, a restrained CRT detail, a monospace technical footer, a chrome-mechanical hint, or a dramatic product-specific color treatment can carry the reference. The product must never become cosplay.

## Anti-references

Do not follow Ableton's website when it uses art direction as the content. Cosimo should show the product and the underlying idea, not replace the product with conceptual editorial photography.

Do not follow Soundtoys when it declares that it has attitude. Avoid cartoon plugin names, red/orange maximalism, knobs with faces, and irreverence performed as a brand claim.

Do not follow Universal Audio when it equates trustworthiness with age. Avoid brushed metal, heritage hardware photography, photoreal hardware-emulation UI, and provenance-first copy.

Do not follow Output when production value substitutes for substance. Avoid moody cinematic screenshots, generic dark UI plus neon accents, and copy pitched down to a casual register.

Do not follow Spitfire Audio when gatekeeping becomes the aesthetic. Avoid institutional prestige signals, black-and-white orchestral documentary styling, and deference to legacy rooms or notational culture as proof of seriousness.

Do not follow ECM Records sleeve language. Avoid washed-out melancholy, faded palettes, and sadness used as a seriousness signal.

Do not follow Arc browser's softness. Avoid pastel friendliness, rounded-everything visuals, and delight-first micro-interactions when functional clarity should carry the experience.

Do not follow generic Swiss-design SaaS minimalism. Avoid huge whitespace, anonymous Inter-driven monochrome, abstract line illustrations, and minimalism used because no product-specific choice was made.

Do not follow FabFilter as an identity model, even though it is respected. Avoid making the whole product feel like an oscilloscope or clinical precision cult. Use technical visuals when they help the task, but leave room for character.

Do not use skeuomorphic plugin design. Avoid photoreal wood grain, brushed-aluminum bezels, decorative screws, and VU-meter styling as costume. Visual elements should express software function, not simulate old objects.

## Design Principles

Encode production judgment. Prefer controls, defaults, labels, and workflows that reveal the founder's producer and mixer expertise instead of exposing raw technical parts for their own sake.

Remove steps without removing control. The interface should collapse common expert workflows while keeping the skilled user in charge of the musical result.

Show the product as the proof. Claims should be backed by working UI, screenshots, diagrams, technical behavior, or concrete features. Do not ask the user to believe a mood.

Make each product a distinct object. The company skin can stay quiet and contemporary, but each plugin or app can carry a committed aesthetic homage when that helps the product's identity.

Fuse craft with futurism. Borrow late-80s and early-90s pop-tech details as precise accents inside modern software. Keep the result subtle, disciplined, and never costume-like.

Respect skilled users. Write for producers who know music theory, modern DAWs, and signal-processing concepts. Do not dumb the product down, but do not force them to do avoidable setup work either.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use readable contrast, clear focus states, keyboard access where the host permits it, touch targets sized for iPhone use, and reduced-motion behavior for animated UI. Color should reinforce meaning, not be the only way to understand state.

The product should support dense expert workflows without relying on tiny labels, low-contrast chrome, or hover-only discovery. When the interface uses visual metaphors from retro computing, manga, chrome futurism, or late-80s pop-tech artifacts, those references should not obscure the actual control model.
